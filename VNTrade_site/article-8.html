The Green Ammonia Transition: Hype vs. Reality

Green ammonia is central to hydrogen economy visions and maritime fuel ambitions — but the timeline and economics remain complex.

Share: in LinkedIn 𝕏 Twitter Email

Ammonia has attracted extraordinary attention in the energy transition narrative. As both a potential zero-carbon maritime fuel and a hydrogen carrier enabling long-distance hydrogen trade, green ammonia has been positioned as a linchpin of the post-fossil energy system. The commodity trade implications — and the gap between projected timelines and commercial reality — deserve careful examination.

Why Green Ammonia Matters

Conventional ammonia is produced via the Haber-Bosch process using natural gas (or coal) as a feedstock — making it one of the most carbon-intensive chemicals produced at scale. Green ammonia, by contrast, is produced using green hydrogen from renewable-powered electrolysis, combined with nitrogen from air separation. The result is ammonia with near-zero lifecycle carbon emissions.

The applications are significant: ammonia as a direct maritime fuel (already in development by major shipping groups and engine manufacturers), ammonia as a hydrogen carrier for international trade, and ammonia's existing use as a nitrogen fertilizer — where green variants could dramatically reduce agricultural Scope 3 emissions for food producers.

The Electrolyser Bottleneck

The fundamental challenge for green ammonia is the same as for green hydrogen broadly: electrolyser capacity. Manufacturing constraints, supply chain bottlenecks and long project development timelines mean that utility-scale green hydrogen production — at costs competitive with fossil alternatives — remains years away at most potential production sites.

Projects in Australia, Chile, Namibia, Saudi Arabia and Oman have all been announced with ambitious production targets. Most face timeline slippage. The economics — green ammonia cost per tonne versus conventional ammonia — remain challenging at current renewable energy costs outside a small number of exceptional resource locations.

Near-Term Realities: Blue Ammonia as a Bridge

In the near-to-medium term, blue ammonia — produced from natural gas with carbon capture — is emerging as the realistic bridge toward green ammonia. Japan and South Korea in particular have entered into supply agreements for blue ammonia from Middle Eastern and Australian producers, viewing it as a pathway to decarbonise industrial and power sector operations while green ammonia supply scales up.

Implications for Conventional Ammonia Trade

The energy transition narrative should not obscure the near-term reality: conventional grey and blue ammonia will dominate physical trade for the foreseeable future. Global nitrogen fertilizer demand is not declining — population growth and yield requirements ensure that.
