Steel Industry Forecast: Decarbonisation Meets Demand Reality

Green steel ambitions are colliding with economic realities — where does that leave bulk iron ore and scrap trade through the decade?

Share: in LinkedIn 𝕏 Twitter Email

The steel industry sits at the intersection of two of the most important forces shaping commodity markets: decarbonisation imperatives and emerging market industrialisation demand. How these forces interact over the coming years will determine the fate of some of the world's largest commodity trade flows — iron ore, coking coal, scrap and DRI/HBI.

Green Steel: The Vision and the Timeline

Green steel — produced using hydrogen-based direct reduction instead of blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace (BF-BOF) technology — has been positioned by European steelmakers and policymakers as the pathway to near-zero emissions steel production. Projects are underway in Sweden, Germany, and across the Middle East. But the timeline to meaningful green steel volumes at competitive cost is longer than early projections suggested.

Green hydrogen — essential for truly green DRI — remains expensive compared to fossil-based alternatives. Electrolyser capacity is scaling but not fast enough to deliver the hydrogen volumes needed for major steel plants before the early 2030s at best. In the interim, the bridge strategy for many mills involves increased scrap use and a shift to natural gas-based DRI rather than hydrogen-based.

Iron Ore Demand: Resilience in the Short-to-Medium Term

Despite decarbonisation ambitions, iron ore demand — particularly for the high-grade lump and pellet feed grades suitable for DRI production — is expected to remain robust through the late 2020s. India's rapidly expanding steel capacity, Southeast Asian industrialisation and continued Chinese output, even at slightly lower levels than peak, provide a substantial demand floor.

The quality split in iron ore is intensifying. Low-grade, high-impurity ores face growing pricing discounts as blast furnace operators tighten quality thresholds and DRI operators require 65%+ Fe content. ECTRADE's iron ore brokerage focuses on serving this quality bifurcation — matching high-grade origins with premium buyers and managing the commercial complexity of increasingly differentiated pricing.

Scrap Metal: The Circular Economy's Commodity

Scrap steel — both ferrous and non-ferrous — is emerging as a critical commodity in the decarbonisation context. Electric arc furnace (EAF) steelmaking, which dominates in the US and is growing elsewhere, uses primarily scrap feed. As global steel production gradually shifts toward EAF, scrap availability and pricing will become increasingly central to the metals supply chain.

Turkey, the world's largest scrap importer, sets benchmark pricing for the global trade. ECTRADE has growing activity in the ferrous scrap segment — connecting collection and trading networks in Europe and North America with EAF steel mills across the Middle East and Asia.

Outlook

The steel decarbonisation transition will reshape commodity trade flows — but more gradually than policy ambitions suggest. In the near term, iron ore and coking coal trade remain substantial. In the medium term, high-grade ore, scrap and DRI gain share. The broker's role in navigating this complex, evolving market is to provide both market intelligence and verified counterparty connections — across the full spectrum of current and emerging steel feedstocks.
